Product Manager  &  Senior Product Manager Candidates
What this guide covers:  A full overview of the assignment, what to look for during the session, the questions you should be asking, and how to score the candidate consistently.
Confidentiality:  Do not share this document with candidates. The assignment brief they receive is separate.

1. What is the Building Assignment?
The building assignment is the third stage in our hiring process for PM and SPM candidates. At this point, we've already validated the candidate's background and operating principles. This stage is designed to answer one question:
Can they actually build? Not spec, not strategise, not describe. Build.
Candidates are given a brief featuring Chico.ai, a fictional B2B SaaS company focused on AI adoption for enterprise customers. They're asked to:
Identify a specific product problem related to AI tool adoption in a commercial business
Form a clear product bet with a deliberate point of view
Build a working prototype or interactive demo using AI tools (Claude, Cursor, Replit, v0, Bolt, etc.)
Present it live to this panel in 30 minutes, and defend their decisions under questioning
The fictional Chico.ai scenario is intentional, it removes context advantage and puts every candidate on equal footing. What we're really evaluating is their thinking process, build velocity, and how they perform under live scrutiny, not how much they know about our specific product.

Any questions you want to probe based on their approach

Product

Engineering

3. What We’re Assessing?
We evaluate candidates across four dimensions. Every panel member scores all four — your perspective from your function adds signal, not noise. Use your instinct.
Dimension 1: Builder Mindset
Interview question to ask
Walk us through how you built this. What did you start with, and how did you use AI tools in the process?
Follow-up probes
Which tools did you use? How did you decide what to build first?
What did you throw away or change direction on mid-build?
How long did the actual build take versus the thinking and planning?

4. Q&A Question Bank
Use the 5-minute Q&A to go deeper on the dimension most relevant to your function, or to probe any gaps you spotted during the presentation. Below are questions you can pull from — you don't need to ask all of them.
Probing the problem framing
You chose this user, what made you rule out other options?
How confident are you that this is actually a top-three pain point for them?
What would change about your solution if the user was a VP rather than an analyst?
Probing the build
Show me the part of the prototype you're least happy with and tell me why.
If you had a full engineering pod for two weeks, what would you prioritise building first?
What's the biggest thing you couldn't get working in the time you had?
Probing the business case
What's the weakest part of your ROI story?
How does this get sold to the economic buyer, the person signing the contract?
What does a competitor's version of this look like, and why is yours better?
Probing decisiveness and ambiguity
If I told you your core assumption was wrong, what would you do tomorrow?
What decision in this process are you least confident about, and why did you make it anyway?
What would you have done differently if you had eight hours less?
Good Q&A practice:
Ask one question at a time. Don't stack questions.
Let silence sit, the best answers often come after a pause.
If an answer is vague, ask: 'Can you be more specific?' rather than moving on.
Pushback is healthy. We're not trying to trip them up, but we do want to see how they handle pressure.
